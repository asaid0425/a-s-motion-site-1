BMW · 02 / 04 — the problem

The starting point: type every search — while driving.

BMW's original in-car search lived on the center-console touchscreen, with an on-screen keyboard.

  • Drivers search each destination, letter by letter.
  • Eyes on the screen and a hand off the wheel.
  • Basic, slow, and distracting in motion.
  • Far from the premium, driver-friendly BMW standard.

The original input — typing a destination on the center-console touchscreen.

questions.

UX motion design for product and entertainment teams — interface motion systems, transitions, microinteractions, AI-native and audio-visual feedback, and cinematic brand moments. The craft of how a product moves, feels, and tells its story.

Every motion that earns its place does a job — gesture confirmation, latency masking, motion affordance, and spatial continuity. Transitions, microinteractions, loading and state feedback, navigation, and cinematic hero moments. If a motion doesn't improve clarity, speed, or feel, it doesn't ship.

A motion system — a standardized duration scale and a small set of easing curves every motion is built from, delivered as tokens. Fewer choices is what makes motion feel like one product and lets engineering implement it without guesswork.

Inside the team, not over the wall. I hand off motion specs engineers can build from, and stay close through implementation so the motion ships the way it was designed.

Hollywood visual effects, then 15+ years in product — Google, LinkedIn, Dropbox, Intuit, and two years in-house at BMW. The film work is why entertainment, content-rich, and interactive-storytelling products feel like home.

Comfortably. I turn fuzzy direction into concrete, visionary concepts fast — then pressure-test them against real users and tighten until the detail is right.

Open to select full-time roles and project work. The fastest start is a 15-minute call.
